Each tip only reads the resource right in front of it — no tip knows the tree. The symmetric half is the attraction that fills space; the skew half is the twist that gives the filling a direction. At skew = 0 the growth is reciprocal and the net chirality only wanders near zero — whatever handedness appears is chance, and a fresh regrow washes it away. That is the V13 result: structure without an arrow. Turn the skew up and something worth noticing happens — the chirality doesn't just rise, it sharpens: a weak local rule leaves the whole shape at the mercy of noise, but a strong one compounds branch after branch into a coherent, repeatable arrow. Small systematic local bias beats large random noise, once it's magnified. That's the V14 fix, and the reason a fern, a galaxy's arm, and a dendritic tree all curl the same way down their whole length. Growth spends only where resource is unmet; once a region is colonized its tips go dark and the cost falls to nothing. Emergence with an arrow, and a price.

What this is: a real space-colonization growth (the algorithm behind leaf venation, coral, and dendritic arbors) with a per-tip chiral bias. Every number is live: node count, active tips, and the net chirality measured from the segments as they form.
What it isn't: a claim that the tree feels its growing. "Resource" and "surprise" are the model's words for gradient and unmet demand, not measured biology.
